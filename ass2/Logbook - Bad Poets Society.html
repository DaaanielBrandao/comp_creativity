Dataset

Because we lack a real background on poetry, especially english poetry, we tried to look for a dataset first in order to understand what it looked like and try to come up with ideas for the generation. The dataset that stood up from the rest was one that contained different folders, each named after the poems theme. This gave us the option to generate similar poems with similar theming. The main drawback of this dataset is the fact that there are various authors with different writing styles which can possibly make the poems feel inconsistent.

Poet background

Since we planned to generate poems by category, generating a poet that goes through different phases of his life writing poems with different themes seems like an interesting way to add background and context to the poems that are going to be generated.

First, we need a name for the poet. We looked into a python library that generates names from a huge database, called ‘names’. It has a distinction between the first and the last name and it’s possible to choose male or female names. To try to give some meaning to the name and not be completely random we tried to make the name slightly similar to the author’s themes. So the first name will be similar to the first theme of their career and the last name to the last theme. In order to do that we generated thousands of names and chose the one that was closer to the theme using a metric that measures similarity between two strings named ‘Jaro distance’ in the jellyfish library. For example for the themes ‘Animal’ and ‘Depression’ the name of the author generated is: Danial Anderson

A biography would also be a good way to explain the phases the author went through. For that, a template that uses a lot o random values was used, including Father’s name, Lover’s name, birthday, the years their life shifted, reason of death, country of origin, etc. The biography always follows a similar structure: Author is born in a country, lives mostly isolated, is exiled and goes to another country, meets the love of their life, they die in a plague, the author spends more time in that country, they plan to come back to their original country, they die while traveling. While the structure stays the same, the reasons for certain events happening, the periods of time, the countries, and the people are randomized and the categories(phases) the author goes through are its inputs.

Generating poems

We started poem generation by playing with Markov chains. These are used to generate new sentences from existing text. So the main idea was to feed the model every poem in a category in order to generate sentences that will fit in our poems. We ended up using the Markovify python library. Another obvious challenge that we had to deal with was rhymes. Python’s Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK) contained a pronunciation dictionary that could help us with that challenge. While searching on the web for generation ideas, we found a neat technique to find sentences that rhymed in a time-efficient way:

  1. Generate a normal and reverse Markov model from the text
  2. Build a sentence using the normal model
  3. Choose the last word and find a rhyming word in the model
  4. Build a reverse sentence using the reverse Markov model, using the word that was found as the starting word
  5. Reverse that verse

This was a way more efficient way to generate rhyming sentences than the naive way of generating a sentence until it rhymes with the previous one. Each sentence also as a lower and upper syllable limit. The way we find rhyming words is the following: First, we use NLTK to see how a word is pronounced, and then find all the existing words in the NLTK pronouncing dictionary that have the same last phonemes. After obtaining every word that rhymes, we choose a word in our model that is in that set of words, if it exists. If it does not exist, we may try a different rhyme by generating a new verse

Using this method we were also able to implement different poems structures: Limerick, Sonnet, and Acrostic. Since the acrostic doesn’t have to have rhyming sentences we only had to worry about the first letter in each sentence.

Presentation

For the presentation, we decided to use a simple Word template found on the internet. Since the biography structure states that the author didn’t publish the earlier work we decided to present this as a modern book that reflects upon the author’s life and has a poem for each phase of his life. The author we are presenting is named Laurel Anderson and writes in the following themes: Nature, Time, Depression. The first one is an acrostic, the second a limerick and the third is a sonnet. For each of the poems presented there is a corresponding illustration. To get a portrait of the author we first used the website https://thispersondoesnotexist.com/ to get a picture of a person that doesn’t exist. This website utilizes StyleGANv2 to generate images of humans that are indistinguishable from real pictures. This is the picture we chose:

After that, we found this website https://ai-art.tokyo/en/ that creates Renaissance-style paintings based on pictures. We are not exactly sure how this works but definitely seems something more elaborate than a simple style transfer. Thankfully we do not have to worry about privacy concerns when submitting the photo since it is not a real person.

Evaluation of the creativity of the system

In order to evaluate the creativity of the system, we are going to use a simplified version of Ritchie’s Empirical Criteria for Attributing Creativity. In short, we are going to evaluate the novelty, quality, and typicality of the system. These are the criteria we are going to use:

  1. What proportion of the total outputs are high quality?
  2. What proportion of the total outputs are novel and typical?
  3. What proportion of the typical outputs are high quality?
  4. What proportion of the novel outputs are typical and high quality?

For the poet background generator, since it’s based on a pre-defined template, there is almost no dissimilarity from the inspiring set. The only novelty comes from the random values, but since the structure of the biography is always the same, it is very low. However, following a strict template makes the output almost always typical and high quality. And sometimes we might even get some unexpectedly funny combinations. With that being said, the proportions for criteria 1 and 3 are very high, while the 2nd criteria score really low. It’s hard to evaluate the 4th criteria since there are no real novel outputs.

As for the poem generator, there is a lot more unexpected and novel outputs, as well as the expected reduction in quality and typicality. After doing some experiments trying to find poems for the poetry book, we found that around 1/10 of them could be considered high-quality. All of the outputs seem novel at a first glance, but it’s hard to evaluate since we don’t know all the poems in the dataset. Since the output tens to stay on theme, almost all of the poems seem typical, so the criteria 1 and 3 should have similar proportions. The 4rth criteria is also hard to evaluate for the same reason as the 2nd.

Moreover, evaluating the quality of a poem is quite difficult, since the way people interpret a poem is different. where someone does not find meaning, someone else may have a personal interpretation of it. The more tangible way we were able to evaluate them was to check the coherence of the sentences and if the theme was noticeably present.
